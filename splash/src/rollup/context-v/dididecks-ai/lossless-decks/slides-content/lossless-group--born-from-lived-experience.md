# Born from lived experience — Michael Staton, Convener

## What this slide says

Lossless wasn't designed in a strategy doc. It was forced into existence by two compounding realities: fifteen years in venture (eight as a GP, with primary responsibility for front-line dealflow and LP fundraising at a firm that scaled from $40M to $1.4B AUM and from 40 to 200 portfolio companies — with no team and no firm-wide systems, only personal productivity and burnout); and three startup foundings, the last of which had to radically cut its team in 2024 just as AI came onto the scene. The cut and the moment posed the same question: *"How could you do the work of 30 with only three people?"* That question is the firm.

## Why this slide exists

The credibility slide proves track record. The methodology slide proves repeatability. **This slide proves the firm isn't an outside observer of the problem it's solving.** The convener has done the GP job from the inside — knows the dealflow inbox, the LP cycle, the firm-wide-systems gap, the cost of being the system-of-record yourself. The firm exists because the founder lived the pain, hit the moment, and asked the question that AI made answerable for the first time.

Job in the arc: turn the deck from a competent pitch into a *real* one. Without this slide, the rest reads as a consulting offer; with it, it reads as a thesis someone is *staking themselves on*.

## Content

### The two compounding realities

**Reality 1 — Inside the GP seat**

- **15 years in venture; 8 as a GP**
- Scaled a firm from **$40M → $1.4B AUM**
- Portfolio grew **40 → 200 companies**
- Primary responsibility: **front-line dealflow and LP fundraising**
- **No team. No firm-wide systems. Only personal productivity and burnout.**

**Reality 2 — Inside the founder seat**

- **Threepeat startup founder.** Three companies. Three rounds of building.
- Last startup, **2024**: forced to radically cut the team — just as AI came onto the scene.
- The cut and the moment posed the same question, in the same week.

### The pivot question

> *"How could you do the work of 30 with only three people?"*

This question is the firm. Everything Lossless does is a working answer to it — for the firms where that question is being asked in earnest.

### Layout candidates

- **Two-act timeline** — GP-era column / founder-era column, with the pivot question spanning the bottom as a pulled-out callout. Reads as biographical, ends on the firm-defining question.
- **Three-band horizontal** — band 1: GP facts, band 2: founder facts, band 3: pivot question in large display type. The question gets visual weight equal to the bio.
- **Quote-led** — open with the pivot question in display type at the top; the two realities underneath as supporting evidence. Strongest if the question carries the slide.
- **Headshot + facts + question** — Michael's photo on the left, the GP and founder facts on the right as terse stats, the pivot question as a footer band. Most conventional; safest for a partner who skims.

### Voice notes

- The numbers ($40M→$1.4B, 40→200) are substantial but they're *supporting evidence for the burnout claim*, not the headline. The slide is about *what it cost to do the GP job without firm-wide systems*, with the scale as the receipt. If the scale becomes the headline, the slide stops being about Lossless and becomes a brag.
- "No team. No firm-wide systems. Only personal productivity and burnout." — three short sentences, declarative. This is the load-bearing line. Don't soften.
- The pivot question is the slide's emotional pivot — it should be set in display type, possibly in Poor Story (the brand's handwriting font), to read as *the actual question he was asking himself in that week*, not as marketing copy.

## Substantiation

- **The GP firm + scale figures** — verifiable via the firm's public materials; should name the firm in speaker notes even if not on the slide
- **The portfolio company count growth** — verifiable; the 40→200 is a strong scale claim that wants a source
- **The threepeat founder claim** — three prior companies; name them (or at least the most recent one) in the body or in speaker notes for credibility
- **The 2024 forced-cut moment** — connect to a date and a public marker if possible (the cut, the AI moment); the temporal coincidence is the load-bearing narrative beat
- **The "30 with 3" question** — note in the body that this is a verbatim question Michael was asking himself in that week; sources the line as authentic rather than retrofitted

For the DD-grade version: the GP firm should be named on slide, and the three prior startups should be at least listed (perhaps in a small footer strip or appendix slide).

## Authoring notes

- **The burnout admission is the most exposed line and the most important.** Keep it. "Only personal productivity and burnout" reads honest because *it's honest*. Softening it ("constant time pressure", "scaling challenges") would gut the slide. The trust the rest of the deck earns comes substantially from this line being on the page.
- **The pivot question is the centerpiece.** Layout has to make it visible — preferably as the largest visual element on the slide. The two realities are setup; the question is the slide's payload.
- **"How could you do the work of 30 with only three people?"** is a question, not a claim. The slide isn't promising 10x productivity; it's surfacing the question that became the firm. That distinction matters — promises of 10x are forecasts, questions are honest, and this slide should not slip into the former.
- **The numbers are receipts, not headlines.** If a designer sets `$40M → $1.4B` in 96pt type, the slide becomes a brag about firm growth. The right visual move: numbers in supporting type, the burnout admission and the pivot question in display type.
- **"Threepeat" is doing real work but is informal.** Audiences vary on whether informal-language credentials read as confident or sloppy. Two safer alternatives if needed: "Three-time founder" or "Three prior startups." Keep "threepeat" if the deck voice is going to be self-aware throughout; switch if the deck wants more formality.
- **The "lived experience" framing is the bridge to methodology.** This slide should pair tightly with the firm-wide-emergent-practices slide — the methodology *is* the answer to the question, applied through discovery rather than prescription. The deck arc benefits from these two being adjacent and visually linked (a common visual motif, a connecting beat in the narration).
- **Convener, not founder/CEO/principal.** "Convener" is a deliberate word — implies bringing people together, surfacing what's already there, not directing from the front. Aligns with the methodology slide's discovery-not-prescription framing. Worth keeping consistent across the deck.
- **Order in the arc.** Almost certainly *before* credibility — the slide arc reads more honestly as origin-story → what-we've-built → how-we-build. Or alternatively, the convener slide is *after* credibility and *before* methodology, working as the pivot ("here's what we shipped, here's why I personally was in a position to recognize the problem, here's how we work"). Either works; the pivot-after-credibility version is probably stronger for an audience that doesn't know Michael yet.

## Open questions

- Is this one slide or a paired set? The GP-era story alone could carry a slide; the founder-era story could carry another; the pivot question could be a transition slide between them. One-slide form is tighter for arc economy but loses the rhythm a two-slide form would have.
- Does the deck want a separate **bio / CV appendix slide** that carries the full Michael track record, freeing this slide to focus narrowly on the pivot question and the burnout admission?
- Should the convener slide come **before or after credibility?** Both orderings work; the order changes what the deck is *primarily about*. Convener-first → the deck is about *the person and what he saw*. Credibility-first → the deck is about *what the firm has shipped*, with the convener as backing evidence. The first reads as a thesis pitch; the second reads as a capability pitch. Worth picking deliberately.
- Should the pivot question be **on this slide or get its own moment** (a single-line, full-bleed transition slide between this one and the methodology one)? "30 with 3" as a transition slide would be a strong visual beat if the deck can afford the air.
- Does the visual treatment include a **Michael Staton headshot**? Conventional for a convener slide; depends on whether the deck wants the human face up-front or whether the question + facts are stronger without one.
