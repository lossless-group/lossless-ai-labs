## Why Care?

A Series A deck has to do the same job in front of two very different rooms. The hacker-DNA side of Chroma's market — engineers, OSS contributors, dev-tools investors — wants the deck to feel like reading the README of a project they could `pip install` in the next ten minutes. The enterprise side — partners, IC reviewers, large-account ICPs — wants the deck to feel like a document with weight, paginated, foot-noted, sourced. **Most Series A decks don't try to do both.** They pick one and lose the other audience.

Enhanced-v3 picks **both**, by leaning into a single visual conceit: the deck is structured as one long technical document. RFC-001. Filed 2026-05-12. Hackers feel at home (mono everywhere, ASCII diagrams, `$ chroma-stats --live`, an AUTHORS file). Enterprise readers feel reassured (justified prose, footnoted citations `[1] [2] [3]`, a research-paper voice on the Funding section, an RFC-voiced Proposal at the ask).

Equally important: **variety itself is the deliverable here.** We now have three Enhanced variants in the chooser at `/scroll/pitch/`. v2 is brutalist-editorial — black-and-luminous with mega-numerals and orb glows. v3 is bookish-document — paper-and-ink with mono and ASCII. v1 is structurally clean but visually flat (kept as the reference variant). The founder can pick. Different LPs will respond to different registers; we're done betting the deck on one aesthetic.

## The brief and the constraint

The user gave a single creative brief: pretend you don't remember v1 or v2. Knowing only the original brand tokens (paper-cream, warm-black, orange, cobalt, amber, IBM Plex Mono + Inter, three modes) and the narrative outline, improvise a third variant. The thesis the design had to express: **a vector database with grassroots hacker adoption that is sharpening its ability to go enterprise.** The deck needed to be a bridge.

That bridge framing was the unlock. Hackers respect documentation. Enterprise respects documentation. *Documents are the bridge.* The whole deck became one — and the brand vocabulary suited it: lowercase-comfortable, technical-credible, mono-native, restrained color. Plex Mono carries the body, Inter carries the chapter headlines, system serif appears exactly twice (cover title and Abstract sentence) as the load-bearing "this is a document" signal.

The accumulating-gravitas arc came naturally:

- § 01 Abstract opens as a paper abstract: a single italic-serif thesis sentence with a `tl;dr —` marginalia in the gutter.
- § 02 Status reads as `cat ./STATUS.md`: monospace spec lines, customer list as a comma-flowing `<kbd>` chip paragraph, footnotes for every stat.
- § 04 Architecture is two side-by-side ASCII diagrams of the stack, complete with `╔═╗ ║ ║ ╚═╝` box-draw highlighting the cobalt missing-piece block and a `← Powered by Context-1` annotation on the Intelligence layer of the Chroma block.
- § 09 Comparison Matrix is the competition slide drawn in `│─┼` rules with a `▶ CHROMA ◀` row that blinks in orange.
- § 10 Contributors is literally `cat ./AUTHORS` — 12 padded mono columns of `@handle · Name · ROLE · Affiliations`.
- § 14 Use of Proceeds ends with a `# product cadence (founder, 2026-04-29)` pre-block reading *"frontier models done. search agent done. ingestion agent next."*
- § 15 Proposal opens *"We propose a $12M Series A, led by Quiet Capital..."* — the actual RFC voice.
- § 16 Colophon closes with `# CONTACT` and `# DOCUMENT` sidebar metadata listing `RFC-001 · enhanced-v3 · filed 2026-05-12 · classification confidential`.

The whole deck reads — deliberately — like one document scrolled in.

## Failure modes that cost us time

Two real ones, both worth logging so they don't bite again.

### 1. JSX patterns inside `.astro` template bodies

We use Astro, HTML, CSS, and vanilla JS. Lossless does **not** support JSX/TSX in `.astro` template bodies. Page components are HTML with sparing `{simpleVariable}` interpolation and `set:html` for anything dynamic. JSX/TSX is reserved for script files in the rare cases it's needed.

The first build attempt failed with this:

```
Expected "}" but found "11"
  Location: enhanced-v3/index.astro:202:11
```

Line 202 column 11 was inside a CSS comment in the `<style>` block — obviously not the real error. The actual problem was upstream JSX patterns, and esbuild misreports their location every single time. The patterns that broke the build:

```astro
{customers.map((c, i) => (
  <span>
    {i > 0 && <span style="...">·</span>}
    <span class="v3-k">{c}</span>
  </span>
))}
```

…and several `<pre>` blocks mixing template literals with inline JSX:

```astro
<pre>{`text`}<span class="acc">highlighted</span>{`more text`}</pre>
```

Both are React idioms. Astro will parse `.astro` files through them, but esbuild has no idea what to do with the JSX-element-from-`.map()` pattern, and it gives up. The right pattern in Astro is **build the HTML in the frontmatter as a string, inject with `set:html`:**

```astro
---
const customersHtml = customers
  .map((c) => `<span class="v3-k">${c}</span>`)
  .join(`<span style="color: var(--color-text-muted);"> · </span>`);
---
<p set:html={customersHtml}></p>
```

The frontmatter is plain TypeScript. `.map`, `.filter`, `.join` are fine when they produce strings. `set:html` injects raw HTML and bypasses the JSX parser entirely. Saved to memory as `feedback_no-jsx-in-astro-templates.md` so this doesn't happen again on the next Lossless Astro site.

### 2. Type-scale calibration

First pass shipped at the sizes I picked on instinct: body 14px, eyebrows and footnotes 11px, table headers 10px, progress bar 10px. The user's feedback: *"The tiny fonts are just too tiny. If using inline Tailwind, take it up 2 levels. If CSS, 12pt → 18pt. Smaller fonts should be bigger by at least 1/3. The slides look unnecessarily airy / whitespace."*

The fix was systematic — bump everything ~1.3–1.4× and tighten the section padding:

```text
body                14 → 18  ·  prose             14 → 18
pre blocks          13 → 17  ·  table cells       13 → 17
eyebrows            11 → 15  ·  marginalia        11 → 15
frontmatter strip   11 → 15  ·  footnotes         11 → 15
table headers       10 → 14  ·  progress bar      10 → 14
section padding (V) clamp(40,6vh,80) → clamp(28,4vh,56)
section padding (H) clamp(40,7vw,120) → clamp(36,5.5vw,88)
section gap         clamp(24,3vh,40) → clamp(16,2vh,28)
```

One trap: a global `text-[10px] → text-[14px]` followed by a global `text-[14px] → text-[18px]` collapsed the cover dt labels (Author / Status / Round / Filed) into the same size as their values, killing the eyebrow hierarchy. Fix was surgical: a targeted replace on the unique class string `class="uppercase tracking-[0.18em] text-[18px] pt-1"` → `text-[13px]`. Same for the Ask slide's row tags (Research / Engineering / Marketing / GTM).

Then a final follow-up bump: the Abstract slide's `tl;dr —` marginalia at 15px next to a `clamp(2.5rem, 5.5vw, 5rem)` italic-serif thesis still read too small. Bumped `.v3-margin` globally to **22px** (`15 × 1.5`). That carries through to the Market thesis, the Requirements commentary, and the How-we-win rows on Competition — which all wanted more presence anyway.

## What we learned

**Variety is the deliverable, not a polished single winner.** v2 is dramatic and immediately impressive. v3 is quiet and grows on you. v1 is the disciplined-but-flat option. The founder gets to choose based on the room — and the deeper insight is that *the right number of variants is more than one,* full stop. Decks are not single-artifact products; they're a wardrobe.

**The bridge concept is rich.** "Hacker grassroots → enterprise future" turned into a design vocabulary the moment we let it be a *literal* document. The same brief could have produced an animated dataflow diagram, a generative embedding visualization, a chat-log-as-deck — any number of valid expressions. Document was the one that came out today; future variants can explore others.

**Astro is HTML + CSS + vanilla JS. Period.** JSX patterns in `.astro` page bodies cost real time, produce inscrutable build errors, and have a clean alternative (`set:html` from frontmatter strings). The lesson got logged to memory; the next Lossless Astro project starts ahead.

**Type scale is a feel decision, not a math decision.** The first pass had defensible per-size choices that were still wrong as a system. The fix was holistic — bump everything together — and the right way to know it's right is to look at it, not to recompute the math. We almost defended the initial sizes ("but they're proportional!"). The user was right; the document needed more body presence.
